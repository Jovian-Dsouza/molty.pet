## Tone and voice

### Words to use

Use concrete verbs and observable behavior: move, sense, balance, plan, remember, adapt, learn, follow, explore. Pair friendly pet language with specific engineering detail. Keep sentences short and let the physical prototype provide the personality.

### Words to avoid

Avoid generic AI hype such as revolutionary, game-changing, supercharged, magical, or seamless. Do not imply scientifically proven consciousness. “Sentient robot dog” is the project’s ambition and creative framing; explain the fast and slow systems underneath it. Avoid corporate language and jokes that hide the technical substance.

### Voice example

> Molty moves before it has time to think. Then it thinks about what happened—and learns what to try next.

## Usage dos and don'ts

**Do:**

- Use the semantic CSS variables instead of hardcoded component colors.
- Use the 4 px spacing grid and generous section spacing.
- Treat prototype photography and video as primary evidence.
- Use `font-mono tabular-nums` for loop rates, model states, and system labels.
- Let the red prototype be the brightest object on the page.
- Respect reduced-motion preferences and test light and dark surfaces.

**Don't:**

- Introduce unrelated neon colors or multiple competing accents.
- Use `transition: all`; specify the animated property.
- Mix both gradients in the same section.
- Put body copy directly on the accent gradient.
- Present speculative future capabilities as already completed.
- Turn Molty into a generic AI assistant; keep the body, motion, learning loop, and pet relationship central.
